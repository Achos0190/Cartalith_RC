# Multi-Scale River Network Research — v0.094

## The Three-Scale Gap

The elevation foundation operates at three distinct scales:

| Scale | Code Path | Resolution | River Representation |
|-------|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| Coarse | `computeFlow(true)` + `streamPowerErode` | 256–1024 px (GW×GH) | Discharge accumulation, MFD drainage |
| Tile LOD | `amplifyRegion` / `refineTile` | Coarse × 2–16× | World-space FBM noise — no knowledge of rivers |
| High-zoom tile | Result of LOD tile | 512–4096 px per tile | ??? |

The gap: when `amplifyRegion` upsamples a coarse tile, it adds **world-space FBM noise tapered by local relief**. That FBM doesn't know where rivers were carved, so at zoom 3–4 the channels become **blurry interpolated smears and FBM can fill them**. Deltas read as broad blurry fans, not distributary networks.

## Research Foundation

### 1. Hydraulic Geometry (Leopold & Maddock 1953, Lacey 1930)

Power-law relationships between discharge Q and channel geometry, empirically derived from ~150 US rivers:

| Variable | At-a-station exponent | At-many-stations exponent |
|----------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Width W | b ≈ 0.26 | b ≈ 0.50 |
| Depth D | f ≈ 0.40 | f ≈ 0.40 |
| Velocity U | m ≈ 0.34 | m ≈ 0.10 |

Continuity constraint: b + f + m = 1.0.

Lacey (1930) regime channels: **W = 1.49·Q^0.5**, D = 0.41·Q^0.3. This is the "at-many-stations" (downstream) scaling — appropriate for procedural generation where we're placing rivers at different positions in a drainage network.

**Procedural implementation (v0.094)**:
```
W_cells = max(1, widthK · sqrt((flow - thresh) / (maxFlow - thresh)))
```
This gives W ∝ Q^0.5 (Lacey downstream scaling), with trunk rivers 4–8 cells wide (high `rel`) and headwaters 1 cell wide.

### 2. AGREE Stream-Burning (Hellweger & Maidment 1997)

The standard DEM conditioning technique for hydrologically consistent multi-resolution terrain:

1. **Sample** known channel locations from a coarse flow accumulation grid
2. **Depress** those locations in the upsampled DEM by a depth proportional to discharge
3. **Apply width** — carve a groove of hydraulic-geometry width

Key properties:
- Applied **after** upsampling, not during (avoids resampling artifacts)
- The stream network comes from the coarse grid → guaranteed to match coarse routing
- Width-aware variant: wider channels (higher Q) burn a wider groove
- Slope-respecting variant: burn maintains monotonic descent downstream (v0.077's `enforceChannelDescent` serves this role for user-drawn rivers; AGREE-burned channels inherit monotonicity from the coarse carved field)

**Seam safety**: Because `burnChannels` samples the same coarse `flowField` at world coordinates, and adjacent tiles are generated by `amplifyRegion` with the same world-coord FBM, the burn is seam-consistent **within the tile interior** (both tiles see the same flow magnitude at any shared coarse coordinate). Burn spreading from off-seam cells does not guarantee Δ=0 at the seam edge, but in practice the seam error is well below 1e-4 unless a high-discharge channel happens to terminate exactly at a tile boundary.

### 3. Multi-Scale Erosion Cascade (Houdini HeightField, ACM TOG 2024)

The gold-standard commercial workflow for multi-resolution terrain:

1. **Coarse erosion** (Feature Size 20+ → valleys, drainage divides, ridge-and-valley landscape)
2. **Upsample** the terrain
3. **Fine erosion** passes (Feature Size 1–5 → channel detail, terrace micro-relief, bank undercutting)

**Key insight**: the coarse pass provides the topological constraint (watershed divides, river confluences, flow network); the fine pass fills in detail *within* that constraint. Running fine erosion first would create channels that are washed away by the coarse pass.

Direct parallel to this codebase:
- Coarse `field` + `flowField` = the constraint (computed at GW×GH)
- `burnChannels` = the fine conditioning pass (applied to upsampled tiles)
- Phase 2 (deferred): per-tile droplet passes using `dropletKernel` for natural terracing

**World Machine failure mode**: per-tile independent erosion without shared boundary conditions breaks river continuity across tile edges (each tile drains independently). The correct approach: burn from the shared coarse flow field first (seam-safe), then optionally run per-tile micro-erosion with coarse-field boundary cells held fixed.

### 4. Delta Distributary Networks (Bolla Pittaluga 2003, Jerolmack & Paola 2007)

Why deltas form and why they emerge naturally from this codebase's existing tools:

**Bifurcation instability** (Bolla Pittaluga 2003): a channel bifurcation is inherently unstable — one branch captures progressively more discharge (winner-takes-all). Stable bifurcations in delta fans require:
- Equal depth in both branches
- Low Froude number (subcritical flow)
- Approximately 70%/30% discharge split for a stable Y-junction

**Avulsion trigger**: a channel avulses (abandons its path for a new one) when the channel bed rises **1–3 m above the surrounding floodplain** (Jerolmack & Paola 2007). In normalized height units this corresponds to a rise of roughly 0.003–0.01 (at 300 m map relief).

**Why deltas emerge naturally here**: `routeSediment` (v0.069) deposits sediment in low-slope coastal zones. `computeFlow` uses MFD (Freeman 1991) which distributes discharge to **all** lower neighbours weighted by slope. In flat coastal terrain, MFD flow naturally spreads to multiple paths — those paths are already in `flowField`. With `burnChannels` carving those multi-paths into LOD tiles, the delta fan resolves into multiple carved channels at high zoom without requiring explicit bifurcation logic.

## Phase Architecture

### Phase 1 — Deterministic Channel Burning (v0.094) ✓

**Approach**: AGREE-style burn seeded from the coarse `flowField`.

**Function**: `burnChannels(tile, W, H, coarseFlow, cW, cH, bounds, sea, opts)`

**Properties**:
- Pure, no globals, headless-testable
- Channel width ∝ Q^0.5 (Lacey)
- Cross-section is parabolic (quadratic falloff — natural valley shape)
- Burn depth ∝ discharge (deeper trunk rivers, shallower headwaters)
- Never raises terrain, never goes below `sea − 0.06`
- Wired into `pyramidTile` via `opts.coarseFlow`, UI toggle "Burn river channels"

**Seam safety**: Trivially satisfied when rivers don't terminate at tile edges (the common case). At exact tile-boundary rivers, seam error < 1e-4 for typical parameters.

### Phase 2 — Per-Tile Micro-Erosion (v0.095) ✓

**Function**: `tileMicroErodeKernel(fld, W, H, P, fixed)` (self-contained, no module globals, worker-ready) + the `tileErode(tile, W, H, opts)` wrapper.

A small droplet pass on the *already-burned* amplified tile adds natural terracing, meanders, and bank texture inside the carved channels (the Houdini upsample-then-fine-erode cascade, §3). The implementation gap the deferral noted — seam continuity — is solved by a **fixed-border mask**: the wrapper pins the outer 1-cell ring (`fixed` Uint8Array), and both `deposit`/`scrape` skip pinned cells, so the tile boundary stays byte-identical to its neighbours'. Because adjacent tiles share that boundary line (refineTile's 1-cell coarse overlap → identical post-burn values), pinning it on both sides keeps **seam-Δ=0 exactly**. The trade-off (acknowledged): sediment can't physically cross the pinned seam, so the erosion *pattern* (not the height) is discontinuous one cell in — acceptable for decorative texturing, not a continuous physical solve. Behind a separate **"Micro-erode tiles"** toggle (slower: ~400 droplets/tile).

**Properties**: pure, deterministic (seeded), all-finite, never NaN; tiny tiles (<4 px) no-op.

### Phase 3 — Delta Channel Sharpening (v0.095) ✓

**Function**: `sharpDelta(tile, W, H, coarseFlow, cW, cH, bounds, sea, opts)`.

Rather than explicit stochastic avulsion (the original Phase-3 sketch), v0.095 takes the simpler, deterministic, seam-safe route that the MFD flow field already enables: in flat coastal terrain MFD spreads discharge across many paths, but `burnChannels`' overlapping burn zones blur them into one broad gradient. `sharpDelta` finds the **coarse-flow local maxima** (the dominant distributary channels — the Bolla Pittaluga 70/30 winners, §4) and carves them an extra `sharpK` deeper, **only in the delta zone** (`tile < sea + zoneH`), restoring channel-vs-floodplain contrast. The local-maximum test runs in **coarse-cell space** (with a 0.1 hysteresis to reject noise), so it is identical for any tile covering that coarse cell → **seam-safe by construction**. Rides the "Burn river channels" toggle (cheap + deterministic).

**Properties**: no-op when flow is zero; only lowers terrain; floor-clamped at `sea − 0.06`; leaves cells above the delta zone untouched; deterministic; seam-Δ < 1e-4.

True stochastic avulsion / lobe-switching over time (animated deltas) remains a possible future extension but is out of scope for a static-map generator.

## Implementation Notes (v0.094)

### Complexity
O(W·H·widthK²) per tile. At 512 px with widthK=3: ~512²·9 ≈ 2.4 M ops. Fast enough for on-demand Refine; not per-frame.

### Default Parameters
| Parameter | Default | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| `thresh` | 0.005 | Minimum relative flow to burn (headwater cutoff) |
| `burnK` | 0.08 | Maximum burn depth at full discharge |
| `widthK` | 3.0 | Width coefficient (W = widthK·√rel cells) |

### What the river-LOD passes do NOT do
- Do not modify the base `field` — all three passes are LOD-only, main map unchanged
- `burnChannels` does not enforce monotonic descent along the channel (coarse carving via `streamPowerErode` handles this)
- Micro-erosion is decorative, not a continuous physical solve across seams (pinned borders)
- No animated lobe-switching / time-evolving avulsion (static-map generator)
